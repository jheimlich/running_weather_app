Master the Tricky In-Between Temperature

40 degrees (5¬∞C) is one of the trickiest temperatures for runners. It's that awkward "in-between" zone - too cold for summer gear but too warm for heavy winter layers. Dress wrong and you'll either freeze for the first mile or overheat by mile two. This guide will help you get it exactly right.

Understanding 40 Degree Running

At 40¬∞F (5¬∞C), you're right at the threshold where your body's response to running dramatically changes. You'll start cold, warm up quickly, and potentially overheat if overdressed. The challenge is finding the sweet spot.

Key Factors at 40¬∞F:
  • You'll warm up 15-20 degrees once running
  • Wind makes it feel significantly colder
  • Humidity affects how cold it feels
  • Your run type matters (easy vs. intense)
  • Personal cold tolerance varies widely

The Golden Rule: The 20-Degree Rule

Dress for 60¬∞F (15¬∞C), not 40¬∞F.

Your body generates significant heat while running. Within 10-15 minutes, you should feel like you're running in 60-degree weather. If you dress for 40¬∞F, you'll be uncomfortably hot halfway through your run.

The Test: You should feel slightly chilly for the first 5-10 minutes. If you're comfortable standing still, you're overdressed.

Standard 40-Degree Running Outfit

The Basic Kit

Top Layer:

  • Long-sleeve technical shirt (moisture-wicking)
  • Optional: Light vest or windbreaker (remove if needed)

Bottom Layer:

  • Running tights OR
  • Running shorts with compression tights

Accessories:

  • Light running gloves (essential!)
  • Headband or light beanie
  • Buff or neck gaiter (optional but nice)

Why This Works: Long sleeves protect your arms from initial cold. Light gloves keep extremities warm (they cool fastest). You can always remove the vest or push up your sleeves if you warm up too much.

Adjusting for Conditions

40¬∞F with Wind (Feels Like 30-35¬∞F)

Wind changes everything at 40 degrees. A 10 mph wind can make it feel like 30¬∞F.

Add:

  • Windproof vest or light jacket
  • Wind-resistant gloves
  • Ear protection (headband or beanie pulled down)

Strategy: Run into the wind first while you're fresh, return with it at your back when you're sweaty.

40¬∞F with Sun (Feels Like 45-50¬∞F)

Sunny 40-degree days feel warmer and are more forgiving.

Wear:

  • Can go with short sleeves if you're a warm runner
  • Definitely just shorts (no tights needed)
  • Light gloves for the start
  • Sunglasses

40¬∞F in Rain (Feels Like 35¬∞F)

Rain at 40 degrees is miserable if you're not prepared.

Wear:

  • Water-resistant jacket (lightweight!)
  • Hat with a brim
  • Water-resistant gloves
  • Running tights (shorts will leave you cold)
  • Moisture-wicking base layer

Pro Tip: Accept that you'll get wet. The goal is staying warm while wet, not staying dry.

Adjusting for Run Type

üèÉ Easy Run or Long Run

You'll run slower and generate less body heat.

  • Standard kit works perfectly
  • Keep light jacket on whole time
  • Warmer gloves if slow pace

üí™ Tempo Run or Workout

High intensity = more body heat.

  • Go lighter - short sleeves might work
  • Shorts are fine (skip tights)
  • You'll warm up very quickly
  • Light gloves to start, remove after warm-up

üèÅ Race Day at 40¬∞F

This is ideal racing weather!

  • Throwaway clothes for start line
  • Race kit: short sleeves, shorts, light gloves
  • Arm warmers you can remove
  • You'll feel cold at start - that's perfect

Common 40-Degree Mistakes

Mistake #1: Overdressing

The Problem: You dress to feel comfortable standing still. By mile 2, you're sweating heavily and uncomfortable.

The Solution: Feel slightly chilly for the first 10 minutes. That's perfect! You'll warm up.

Mistake #2: Skipping Gloves

The Problem: "It's not that cold, I don't need gloves." Then your fingers go numb.

The Solution: Always bring light gloves for 40¬∞F. Hands and ears cool fastest. You can always remove gloves and put them in your pocket.

Mistake #3: Cotton Anything

The Problem: Cotton holds moisture. Once you start sweating, it stays wet and cold.

The Solution: 100% technical fabrics. Always. At any temperature.

Mistake #4: Ignoring Wind Direction

The Problem: You start with wind at your back, feeling great. Return into wind, now sweaty, and freeze.

The Solution: Start into the wind, return with it. This is crucial at 40¬∞F.

Post-Run: Don't Catch a Chill

At 40¬∞F, you can cool down dangerously fast after stopping.

Immediately after finishing:

  • Remove wet layers
  • Put on dry, warm clothing (have it ready!)
  • Get inside within 10-15 minutes
  • Warm shower helps recovery

Car runners: Keep a complete dry outfit in your car. Sitting in wet clothes at 40¬∞F is how you get sick.

The Bottom Line

40 degrees is goldilocks running weather - not too hot, not too cold. Once you dial in your outfit, it's one of the best temperatures for running. The key is fighting the urge to overdress and trusting that you'll warm up.

Remember:

  • Dress for 60¬∞F, not 40¬∞F
  • Always bring gloves
  • Layer smart (things you can remove)
  • Embrace being slightly chilly at the start
  • Technical fabrics only
